What is FOIA?

Since 1966, the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) has granted the public the right to request access to records from any federal agency. Under FOIA, federal agencies are required to disclose any information requested unless it falls under a specific set of exemptions or exclusions.

The public's right to request records has served as the backbone for information disclosure in the United States. Records released under FOIA have led to the publication of many important news stories and greater public awareness around government activities.

Why would I make a FOIA request?

While the Federal Government makes a lot of information available in many places, you might still want to access information that has not already been publicly released. FOIA makes it possible to access records that are not yet publicly available.

What can I ask for?

You can file a FOIA request for any “agency record.” This is a broad term that covers things like books, papers, maps, photographs, emails, machine-readable materials, and other documentary materials.

You can also specify whether you would like to receive these records in electronic or paper formats as well. Not all records are available in both formats, but agencies honor record format requests when they can.

Is there a special form I have to use to make a FOIA request?

You do not need to use a specific form to make a request. All you need is to do is write a description of the information you are seeking and comply with any agency-specific requirements, which are described in agencies' FOIA regulations. Once you've written your description and made sure your request complies with requirements, you can send it to the appropriate agency.

Your written description must be specific enough that agency FOIA professionals can understand type of information you're seeking and properly conduct a search. Information such as time periods, specific names, topics, or keywords related are all helpful to the agency representatives who will search and process your request. It's a good practice to make your request as specific as you can.

How do I make a FOIA request?

To make a FOIA request, write a complete and detailed description of the information you're seeking and submit it to the agency you'd like the information from. You may submit your request electronically (via email) or in paper format.

The FOIA does not request agencies to do research for you, analyze data, answer written questions that are unrelated to your request, or create records in response to your request. That means you may need to do some research before you file your request. An agency's own website is a good place for you to start researching how best to write your request; you may find that the information you're looking for is already available on the agency's site.

What happens after I make my FOIA request?

A lot happens behind the scenes after an agency receives your FOIA request. Each agency has its own specific way of processing requests. The first step an agency will take is to determine which offices may have responsive records and then search for those records. Once an office has located records and provided them back to the FOIA shop, the FOIA team may consult subject matter experts, lawyers, or other agency officials to review them for release. This step may also include redacting any non-public information. Once the agency has completed this process, any public records will be released to you.

How long does it take to fulfill my request?

How quickly you receive your information depends on what you've requested (and from what agency). Response time varies among agencies and also depends on the complexity of your request; more complicated requests can take longer to fulfill. In general, most requests are responded to within 20 business days.

Agencies may also request an extension in unusual or exceptional circumstances and may pause the request clock to clarify the request. If an agency gets an extension, it will notify you. During this time, the agency will provide you the chance to modify the scope of your request to help improve the response time. If you're comfortable making your request less complex, you may be able to access your requested information more quickly.

If you're not comfortable changing the scope of your request, you have the option to agree to a different timetable for processing the request. You can work with the agency's FOIA Public Liaison or other FOIA contacts to discuss this option.

If all cases, you should feel free to contact the agency to check on the status of your request. Agencies are required to provide an estimated response date upon request.

What happens if I’m not satisfied with the response I received?

If you disagree with agency’s decision on your request — for example, if you don’t receive as much information as you initially requested — you can file an appeal by writing to the agency. When an agency receives an appeal, it will conduct an independent review to make sure your request was properly addressed. After completing this review, the agency will let you know whether your request has been granted or denied. FOIA requires that appeal requests receive a response within 20 business days, but response times can vary based on an agency’s workload and the complexity of your appeal.

Sometimes an appeal isn’t the best route. Before filing an appeal, you might want to try contacting the agency that handled the request. You can reach out directly to your FOIA contact, or you can contact the agency’s FOIA public liaison, who will offer guidance throughout the FOIA process and help resolve any disputes. Every agency provides information about their FOIA public liaison on their own page or component pages.

If the appeal process is complete and you’re still not satisfied with the result, you can also seek mediation services from the FOIA Ombudsman’s office, the Office of Government Information Services at the National Archives and Records Administration.
